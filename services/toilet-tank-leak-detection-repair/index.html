A puddle behind a toilet does not always mean the base. The tank itself has several places to leak, and they sit above the bowl rather than at the floor. Water can escape where the tank bolts to the bowl, through the gasket between them, from the flush valve inside, or through a hairline crack in the porcelain. And sometimes the tank is not leaking at all, it is sweating. Telling a real leak from condensation is the first step.

The connection

Tank-to-bowl bolts and gasket

The tank sits on the bowl on a thick spud gasket and is held by two mounting bolts. Over years the rubber on the bolts and the gasket hardens, and the tank weeps where it meets the bowl, dripping down the back and pooling behind. It looks like a base leak but starts higher up. Replacing the bolt seals and the tank-to-bowl gasket is a contained repair that does not touch the floor seal at all.

Inside the tank

Flush valve, overflow, and fill valve

Inside, the flush valve and its seal release water to the bowl on a flush, and a leak there lets the tank drain down so the fill valve keeps refilling. The overflow tube and the fill valve have their own failure points. La Mirada's hard imported water leaves scale on all of these parts, stiffening seals until they no longer seat. A dye check in the tank, watching for color to slip into the bowl, confirms an internal leak before any part is replaced.

Leak or sweat

When the tank is only condensing

In the warm months, cold water in the tank can pull the tank's surface below the dew point, and humid bathroom air condenses on the porcelain and runs to the floor. It mimics a leak exactly, a wet base with no failed part. We confirm it by drying the tank and watching whether the water returns on the outside or from a seal. The fix there is not a repair but an anti-sweat measure, which is worth knowing before any parts are swapped.

Stopping the sweat

When the fix is keeping the tank dry

A tank that condenses rather than leaks does not need new parts, it needs to stop reaching the dew point. An insulating liner kit fitted inside the tank, or a mixing valve that nudges the incoming water temperature up a little, keeps the porcelain warm enough that humid air no longer beads on it. Better bathroom ventilation helps too, since drier air condenses less. We point to the measure that fits, because swapping a flush valve does nothing for a tank that was only sweating.

Cracks and cost

The one that means a new tank

A hairline crack in the porcelain, above or below the water line, is the one tank leak that parts cannot fix. A crack only grows, so a cracked tank is replaced rather than sealed. Everything else, the bolts, the gasket, the flush valve, the fill valve, is inexpensive parts and a short visit. Questions we hear

Answered for La Mirada homeowners

Water is behind my toilet but the base looks dry. Why?

The tank has several leak points above the bowl: the tank-to-bowl bolts and gasket, the flush valve inside, or a cracked tank. Any of these drips down the back and pools behind, looking like a base leak.

What is the tank-to-bowl gasket?

It is the thick seal between the tank and the bowl, with two mounting bolts. As the rubber hardens, the tank weeps where it meets the bowl. Replacing the gasket and bolt seals is a contained fix.

Why is my toilet tank wet on the outside?

It may be sweating, not leaking. Cold water cools the porcelain below the dew point and humid air condenses on it. We confirm by drying the tank, since the fix is an anti-sweat measure, not a part.

Can a cracked tank be repaired?

No. A hairline crack in the porcelain only grows, so a cracked tank is replaced rather than sealed. Other tank leaks are inexpensive parts.

Does hard water affect the tank parts?

Yes. La Mirada's hard imported water scales the flush valve, fill valve, and seals until they no longer seat cleanly, which is a common cause of an internal tank leak.
