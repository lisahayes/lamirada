Open the cabinet under a leaking sink and you are looking at three systems in one tight space: the supply lines with their shutoff valves, the drain with its trap, and the underside of the faucet. Any of them can wet the cabinet floor, and the water pools in the same low spot no matter which failed. The work is telling them apart before the particleboard swells and the cabinet bottom gives way.

The suspects

Three sources, one wet spot

A pressurized supply line or its shutoff valve drips steadily, wet whether or not the sink is in use. A drain or trap weeps only while water runs and stops after. The basket strainer that seats the drain in the sink can lose its seal and let water down the back of the bowl. In a kitchen, the garbage disposal adds a seam that can leak, and a worn sprayer hose adds another. The pattern of when it is wet tells us where to look first.

Kitchen and bath

What is different under each

A bath vanity is usually the simpler cabinet: two supply lines, a trap, and a faucet. A kitchen adds the disposal, the sprayer, and often a second basin with its own strainer and drain, which means more joints and more suspects in the same space. Either way the damage lands on the cabinet floor, and in older homes around Downtown La Mirada and the streets near the La Mirada Theatre, that floor is often particleboard that swells fast once it is wet.

Detection and repair

Dry it, test each system, fix the source

We dry the cabinet completely, then run each system in turn: pressurize and watch the supply connections, fill and drain to test the trap and strainer, run the disposal and sprayer in a kitchen. Whichever wets first is the source. From there it is a new supply line or valve, a reseated strainer, a trap repair, or a disposal fix. We also dry and check the cabinet itself, because a leak left long enough damages the wood as much as the plumbing.

After the fix

Drying the cabinet so it lasts

Stopping the leak is half the work under a sink. The other half is the cabinet, because a wet base shelf grows mold on the underside and the plywood or particleboard keeps wicking water even after the plumbing is dry. We pull what is stored, dry the base, and check whether the shelf has swelled or gone soft. A cabinet caught early dries out and stays sound. One left under a slow leak for months is the part that ends up needing a carpenter, which is the real argument for calling at the first damp towel.

A common misconception

Small leaks can wait

For a sink scenario, a pinhole losing a quart per hour translates to 5,500 gallons a year sitting in the subgrade under the foundation. The soil holds it, the concrete flexes over it, and next year runs the same math.

What actually confirms it

A meter reading with every fixture off is the direct confirmation. If the dial moves, the loss is real.

Questions we hear

Answered for La Mirada homeowners

Why is the cabinet under my sink wet but I cannot see the leak?

Three systems share that cabinet, and they all drip to the same low spot. We dry it and run each in turn to see which wets first, rather than guessing from the puddle.

How do I tell a supply leak from a drain leak under the sink?

A supply leak is wet constantly, since the line is pressurized. A drain or trap leak appears only while water runs and stops after. The timing points to the source.

Can the basket strainer leak?

Yes. The strainer that seats the drain in the sink can lose its seal and let water run down the back of the bowl into the cabinet. Reseating it fixes that.

My kitchen sink leaks more than a bathroom one. Why?

A kitchen cabinet has more in it: a disposal, a sprayer hose, and often two basins with their own drains. More joints means more places to leak in the same space.

Will the cabinet need replacing?

Only if a leak ran long enough to swell the particleboard floor. Caught early, the plumbing fix is small and the cabinet is fine, which is why timing matters.
