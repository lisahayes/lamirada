The water line is the artery of a house. It carries pressurized drinking water from the meter to every tap, and when it springs a leak the symptoms reach the whole home at once. Pressure falls off at every fixture, the meter never quite stops, and the bill climbs without one room to blame. That whole-house pattern is what marks a main supply leak apart from a single dripping fixture.

Where the supply fails

The line between the meter and the house

The stretch that carries water from the meter into the home takes constant pressure, and it ages. Older supply lines of galvanized steel scale and narrow until they weep. Aging poly or copper can give out at a fitting or a corroded length. Because this line serves everything, a leak here does not stay polite, and high incoming pressure only hurries it along. In North La Mirada and the established streets near Splash La Mirada, this run is often the oldest pipe on the property.

Detection

The meter tells the first half of the story

We start with a meter test. With every fixture off, a moving meter confirms a leak. Then we shut the valve at the house and watch again: if the meter keeps moving, the leak sits between the meter and that valve, on the supply line itself. A pressure gauge reads how far the line has dropped, and acoustic listening tracks the escape along the run. That isolation is what separates a supply leak from a problem inside the walls.

Repair

Restore the supply, fix the pressure

The repair targets the failed length of the supply line, with a spot repair where the line is otherwise sound or a replacement of the run where the material has aged out. An old galvanized supply line that has scaled shut is usually worth replacing whole rather than patching, since the next weak point is rarely far behind. We also check the pressure regulator, because incoming pressure that runs too high shortens the life of whatever we install.

When it gets urgent

What a failing main does to a home

A supply line near the end of its life can do more than drop the pressure. Taps sputter as air gets pulled in, the water runs cloudy or rust tinged as a scaling line sheds, and in a bad break the home can lose water entirely. If that happens, the shutoff at the house stops the flow inside, and the curb stop out at the street can be closed in an emergency. Knowing where both valves sit before a break is worth a minute on a calm day, and we point them out on every supply call.

Questions we hear

Answered for La Mirada homeowners

What are the signs of a main water line leak?

Pressure that dropped across the whole house, a meter that keeps moving with every fixture off, and a bill that rose without an obvious source. The whole-house pattern is the giveaway.

How do you tell a supply line leak from an indoor leak?

The meter test. We shut the house valve and watch the meter. If it keeps moving, the leak is on the supply line between the meter and the house, not inside the walls.

Is the main water line the city's responsibility?

Only up to the meter. Suburban Water Systems serves the meter, and the line from there into your home is the homeowner's to repair.

Should an old galvanized supply line be replaced or patched?

Usually replaced. Once galvanized steel scales and weeps, the next failure tends to follow soon, so replacing the run lasts longer than a patch.

Does water pressure matter?

Yes. Incoming pressure that runs too high shortens the life of a supply line and its fittings, so we check the regulator as part of the repair.
