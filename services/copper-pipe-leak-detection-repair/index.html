Copper was the standard supply pipe when most of La Mirada was built, prized for lasting decades. Decades is the key word. The copper laid in the 1950s and 1960s tracts near Olive Lane has carried hard imported water long enough to corrode from the inside, and copper fails in a few signature ways: the pinhole that weeps, the green crust at a solder joint, and formicary corrosion that riddles a length of pipe with microscopic tunnels.

How copper fails

Corrosion, joints, and velocity

Copper does not rot like steel, it corrodes in patterns. Aggressive water etches the inner wall until a pinhole opens. Solder joints give out where flux was left to keep eating the metal. On hot lines and tight bends, water moving fast scours the pipe thin, a wear called erosion corrosion. And formicary corrosion, driven by certain chemistry, tunnels a maze through the wall that leaks long before the pipe looks bad. Each leaves a different clue at the surface.

Detection

Reading the copper

Exposed copper tells on itself. Green or white salts crusting at a joint or along a length mark where water has been weeping. For hidden runs, acoustic listening catches the spray and moisture meters trace the spread. Knowing where the original copper runs in an Adelfa Park or Olive Lane home points the search at the lines most likely to be failing, since copper of the same age tends to go in the same places.

Repair

Solder a section, or plan a repipe

A single corroded spot on otherwise sound copper is cut out and a new section soldered in. The harder call comes when a home's copper is failing in several places, because the same water and the same age act on all of it at once. At that point a planned repipe of the failing runs, sometimes converting to PEX, costs less over time than soldering one leak after another. We base that call on what the copper shows, not on a default.

Water chemistry

Why the same homes, the same spots

Copper does not fail at random, it fails where the water works hardest on it. Hot recirculation lines see the most flow and wear thin first. Chloramine, the disinfectant in the imported supply, is part of the chemistry that pits the metal. Blue or green staining in a tub or sink is copper dissolving upstream, a hint the pipe is shedding before it ever leaks. Reading those signs across a block of same era homes is why a leak in one Olive Lane house often means a neighbor is not far behind. It is also why we will tell you plainly when a single repair is buying time rather than fixing the run for good.

Sixty years of hard water

The imported blend that serves La Mirada runs hard, around 9 to 12 grains per gallon, and that mineral load is exactly what wears copper from within. Sixty years of it is why the original tracts see these leaks now. A single solder repair is a contained cost, while a repipe of failing runs is a larger, planned project. Questions we hear

Answered for La Mirada homeowners

Why does copper pipe corrode here?

Hard imported water, around 9 to 12 grains per gallon, etches copper from the inside over decades. The 1950s and 1960s copper in older La Mirada homes has had sixty years of it.

What is formicary corrosion?

A pattern that tunnels microscopic mazes through the copper wall. The pipe can leak while still looking sound on the outside, which is why detection matters.

Should I repair the copper or repipe?

A single failure on healthy copper is a simple solder repair. When the copper is failing in several places, a planned repipe, sometimes to PEX, usually costs less over time.

Is a pinhole the same as a copper pipe leak?

A pinhole is one way copper fails. Copper also leaks at solder joints and from erosion on fast hot-water runs, so we look at the whole material, not just one hole.

Can copper be converted to PEX?

Yes. When repiping failing copper runs, PEX is a common, durable replacement that stands up well to hard water.
